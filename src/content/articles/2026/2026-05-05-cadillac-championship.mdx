The Blue Monster showed its teeth all week, and it was Cameron Young who handled them best. His second PGA Tour victory came at Trump National Doral in a week where the cream largely rose to the top — Scottie Scheffler lurking in second, Adam Scott proving he can still contend with the best of them at 45.

## The Recap

Young has always had the raw talent to stack weeks like this. The power, the ball-striking, the willingness to take on a golf course rather than manage his way around it. What's been missing, historically, is the ability to close — the kind of quiet, ruthless weekend golf that turns near-misses into trophies. At Doral, he found it. With Scheffler breathing down his neck, Young didn't flinch. That's the development that matters more than any swing change or equipment tweak.

Scheffler's runner-up finish is almost comically predictable at this point. The man simply does not have bad weeks. Ben Griffin continued his steady ascent with a T3 showing, and Adam Scott — whose Wikipedia page hilariously redirects to the Parks and Recreation actor — reminded everyone that age is mostly a number when your swing was built to last. Sepp Straka and Si Woo Kim rounded out a quality top five, while the cluster of Alexes (Noren, Smalley, Fitzpatrick) made the middle of the leaderboard feel like a roll call at a British prep school.

## What Actually Happened

**Cameron Young's trajectory finally bends upward.** Young's talent was never in question — he was a PGA Tour winner already, he'd contended in majors, he had the length to overpower most courses. But there was a stretch where you wondered if the close calls were starting to calcify into something permanent. This win at Doral suggests otherwise. Two titles now, and the second one came under genuine pressure from the best player on the planet.

**Scheffler's consistency borders on absurdity.** Another top-two finish. Another week where he was never out of it. Scheffler, a four-time major champion, has essentially made contention his baseline. The gap between him and the field isn't always visible in final scores, but it's there in the way he never gives back holes in bunches. He's the tide — always there, always rising.

**Adam Scott's quiet resurgence.** A T4 finish at Doral is noteworthy for a player who turned professional in 2000. Scott's longevity owes everything to a swing that has barely changed in two decades and an iron game that still holds up against players fifteen years his junior. He's not chasing distance. He's not reinventing himself. He's just playing golf the way he always has, and it still works.

**Ben Griffin belongs in the conversation.** Griffin's name doesn't generate the buzz of some of his peers, but a T3 at a strong-field event isn't an accident. He's building a body of work that suggests he's not a flash in the pan — he's a player who consistently puts himself in the right positions on weekends.

## Course & History

Trump National Doral's Blue Monster has been a fixture on Tour for decades, though its relationship with the PGA Tour has been complicated in recent years. The course demands length off the tee and precision into firm, well-guarded greens — a combination that tends to reward the most complete players in the field. The water on 18 remains one of the more dramatic finishing holes on Tour, the kind of closer that can turn a comfortable lead into chaos in sixty seconds.

The Cadillac Championship (under various sponsor names) has been played at Doral since the 1960s, and the list of past champions reads like a who's who of the game. It's the kind of venue where the course itself is a character in the story — you don't win at Doral by accident.

## Looking Ahead: Truist Championship

The Tour heads to Quail Hollow Club in Charlotte, one of the most demanding tests on the annual schedule. The Green Mile — holes 16, 17, and 18 — is as brutal a closing stretch as exists in professional golf. It asks players to hit precise tee shots on demanding par 4s, navigate water and thick rough, and make putts under pressure that feels heavier than the moment should allow. This is a big-boy golf course: long, tight, and with bermuda greens that can get slippery and unpredictable by Sunday afternoon.

Quail Hollow rewards length off the tee — you need it just to keep up — but the real separator is approach play into well-protected greens and the ability to scramble when you miss. The course fit data this week skews heavily toward power players who can also manage their iron games, which is exactly what you'd expect from a venue with this much teeth.

Rory McIlroy is the obvious storyline. Quail Hollow is his adopted home course, the place where he's had some of his best results, and the data backs up what the eye test suggests — he's the No. 2 course fit in the field. Cameron Young rides in on the momentum of a win, ranked No. 3 in the world by DataGolf and No. 12 in course fit. And then there are the names you might not expect: Aldrich Potgieter tops the course fit rankings, Nicolai Højgaard sits at No. 3, and Chris Gotterup checks in at No. 4. Quail Hollow has a way of surfacing players whose games match its demands, regardless of their name recognition.
