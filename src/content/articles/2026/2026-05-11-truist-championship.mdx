The first major championship of the year had to wait a week, and when it arrived, the warm-up act at Quail Hollow turned out to be its own kind of drama. Kristoffer Reitan — a 26-year-old Norwegian who had never won on the PGA Tour — put together four rounds that nobody in a loaded field could match, finishing at -15 to claim the Truist Championship by two shots over Rickie Fowler and, fittingly, his countryman Nicolai Højgaard.

## The Recap

Reitan's win is the kind that makes you reconsider what you thought you knew. He entered the week ranked 43rd in DataGolf's world rankings, with a course fit profile that suggested Quail Hollow suited him — but nobody was circling his name in Sharpie. What he did was methodical: he found the fairways, kept the ball below the hole on Quail Hollow's treacherous green complexes, and never gave the chasers a real opening.

Fowler's week will generate the most conversation. A tie for second at -13 is the latest chapter in what's been a quiet resurgence — the kind of consistent, unspectacular golf that doesn't trend on social media but shows up on leaderboards. Højgaard matched him there at -13, continuing to look like a player whose ceiling is significantly higher than his current ranking suggests.

Alex Fitzpatrick — the younger brother of Matt — grabbed solo fourth at -12, a significant result for a player still establishing himself on Tour. J.J. Spaun rounded out the top five at -11. Further down, the names get interesting: Tommy Fleetwood, Sungjae Im, Ludvig Åberg, Harry Hall, and Patrick Cantlay all crammed into the T6-T10 range, which tells you the quality of the field even with the PGA Championship lurking seven days away.

Elsewhere, Yurav Premlall won the Estrella Damm Catalunya Championship on the DP World Tour, and Lucas Herbert took the LIV Golf Virginia event over Sergio García and Bryson DeChambeau.

## What Actually Happened

**Reitan proved the model right — sort of.** His DataGolf course fit ranking was 13th, which is good but not screaming "back the truck up." What it does suggest is that Quail Hollow rewards a specific kind of player — long enough to manage the par 5s, accurate enough to avoid the tree-lined disasters, and composed enough to putt on surfaces that punish indecision. Reitan checked every box. The gap between course fit models and actual results often comes down to putting, and this week, his putter was the difference.

**Fowler's game is trending in the right direction at the right time.** A T2 finish the week before the PGA Championship is the kind of form line that would've meant everything to Rickie three years ago, when he was struggling to keep his card. Now it's a quiet confirmation that the swing changes have stuck. Whether he can sustain it across four days at the same venue next week is the question everyone at Quail Hollow will be asking.

**Højgaard continues to outperform his ranking.** At 20th in the DataGolf world rankings but 3rd in course fit for Quail Hollow, his T2 finish is exactly the kind of result the numbers predicted. He's a ball-striker who thrives on courses that demand length and precision off the tee — Quail Hollow is precisely that course. The PGA Championship staying at the same venue is a significant detail.

**The Fitzpatrick brothers both matter at this venue.** Alex finishing fourth while his older brother Matt — ranked 4th in the world by DataGolf — prepares for the PGA Championship at the same course is a subplot that writes itself. Matt's course fit rank (23rd) is notably lower than his overall ranking, but watching his brother tear up the same greens might tell him something useful.

**Cantlay's T10 is easy to overlook and shouldn't be.** His course fit ranking (28th) isn't inspiring, but his world rank (9th) reflects a player whose baseline is so high that even a mediocre fit produces results. At a major championship, that consistency becomes more valuable, not less.

## Course & History

Quail Hollow Club has hosted this event since 2003, back when it was the Wachovia Championship. The course opened in 1961 as part of a private member club in Charlotte's Quail Hollow neighborhood, and over six decades it's evolved into one of the PGA Tour's most demanding tracks. The Green Mile — the closing stretch of holes 16, 17, and 18 — has become one of the most recognized finishing sequences in professional golf.

What makes next week extraordinary is that the PGA Championship returns to this same venue. Players who competed this week essentially got a four-round scouting report. Those who didn't — and that includes several major contenders who skipped the Truist — are operating with less current intel on green speeds, rough density, and pin positions. Whether that advantage is meaningful or marginal is one of the week's most interesting debates.

The tournament has been through several name changes — Wachovia Championship, Quail Hollow Championship, Wells Fargo Championship, and now Truist Championship — but the bones remain the same: a demanding, tree-lined course in the Carolina heat that tends to produce deserving winners.

## Looking Ahead: PGA Championship

The PGA Championship at Quail Hollow Club. The same venue, seven days later, with the stakes multiplied by a factor of about fifty.

The obvious storyline: players who just competed in the Truist Championship have course knowledge that's as fresh as it gets. Reitan, Fowler, Højgaard, Åberg, Cantlay — they all know exactly how these greens are rolling, where the pins can go, and which misses are survivable. That's not nothing.

The less obvious storyline: Quail Hollow in PGA Championship configuration will be a different animal. Expect narrower fairways, thicker rough, firmer greens, and pin positions designed to test nerve rather than reward aggression. The Green Mile, already brutal in a regular Tour event, becomes a genuine examination of character under major championship pressure.

The field will be dramatically stronger. Scottie Scheffler, Rory McIlroy, Xander Schauffele, Collin Morikawa — the names who skipped this week will all be present. The question is whether local knowledge or raw talent wins the argument.
