The weekend belonged to Cameron Young, who has spent the better part of two years looking like a player whose talent far exceeded his trophy case. That gap got a little smaller at the Cadillac Championship.

## The Recap

Young put together the kind of week that felt less like a breakthrough and more like an inevitability finally arriving. With Scottie Scheffler lurking in second, this wasn't a case of the field rolling over — Young had to earn it against the best player on the planet. Ben Griffin grabbed third, Adam Scott continued his quietly excellent 2025 with a fourth-place finish, and Sepp Straka rounded out the top five. Si Woo Kim, Alex Noren, Alex Smalley, Kurt Kitayama, and Alex Fitzpatrick — whose older brother Matt might want to start taking notes — filled out the top ten.

What stood out wasn't just the result but the composure. Young is the kind of bomber who can look lost when the driver goes sideways, but this week he paired that length with the kind of short-game precision that makes a leaderboard hold up on Sunday. Scheffler, for his part, did what Scheffler does — showed up near the top with a seemingly effortless gear that's become background noise for the rest of the Tour. The fact that it wasn't enough tells you something about how well Young played.

## What Actually Happened

**Cameron Young has figured out the missing piece.** His two PGA Tour wins now represent a player who's learned to close. For a long time, Young's narrative was "most talented guy without enough hardware." He was a runner-up machine, a guy who'd dazzle for 54 holes and then let the Sunday tension tighten his grip. That story is changing. Winning with Scheffler on your hip — the world number one, owner of four major championships — is not something that happens by accident.

**Scheffler's seconds are more interesting than most players' wins.** He finished runner-up and nobody was surprised, which is its own kind of absurd. The man has warped expectations so thoroughly that a second-place finish barely registers as a storyline. But it's worth noting: he's still accumulating these near-misses at a rate that suggests the next win is never far away. He's not slumping. He's just running into players having the best week of their lives.

**Adam Scott is defying the calendar.** A fourth-place finish at this stage of his career isn't nostalgia — it's production. Scott has always had one of the most aesthetically pure swings in the game, but aesthetics don't cash checks. The fact that he's still mixing it up in top-fives against this generation of players says something about the longevity of sound fundamentals.

**The Fitzpatrick family business expands.** Alex Fitzpatrick, younger brother of Matt, posted a top-ten finish that suggests the surname might carry some genetic advantage. He's still early in his PGA Tour career, but weeks like this are how you build a schedule and a reputation.

**Kurt Kitayama keeps showing up.** A T9 here after contending recently — Kitayama is one of those players who's consistently in the conversation without getting the spotlight. He's the kind of steady presence that makes him dangerous in any format, any week.

## Course & History

The Cadillac Championship carries a name that evokes the Doral days, the Blue Monster, Trump's fingerprints all over the clubhouse carpet. The tournament has moved through various iterations and sponsors, but its DNA has always been about rewarding complete players — guys who can drive it, shape it, and putt under pressure. The field this week reflected that. This isn't a course that lets one-dimensional players hang around. Length helps, but only if you can navigate the scoring zones with precision.
