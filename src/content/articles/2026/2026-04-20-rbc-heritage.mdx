The week after Augusta always carries a strange energy — half the field is still processing what happened at the Masters, and the other half is grateful they don't have to think about it anymore. At Harbour Town, Matt Fitzpatrick reminded everyone that his game travels just fine from major venues to Lowcountry charm, while across the border in Playa del Carmen, Jon Rahm continued to look like a man intent on making LIV Golf relevant whether you want it to be or not.

## The Recap

**RBC Heritage:** Fitzpatrick's win at Harbour Town feels like one of those results that makes perfect sense in retrospect but nobody really saw coming. This is a course that rewards precision over power, placement over pyrotechnics — and Fitzpatrick has built an entire career on exactly that. He held off Scottie Scheffler, who once again proved he can contend anywhere he tees it up, even on a week when most Masters contenders are running on fumes. Si Woo Kim's third-place finish was a quiet reminder that he's one of the more talented ball-strikers on Tour when his putter cooperates. Collin Morikawa lurking in fourth feels inevitable at this point — the man simply does not miss cuts at courses that demand iron play. Further down the board, Rickie Fowler in eighth and Gary Woodland rounding out the top ten added some crowd-pleasing storylines to a week that could have easily been a post-Masters sleepwalk.

**LIV Golf Mexico City:** Rahm won. Again. At this point, his LIV results read less like tournament finishes and more like an ongoing argument with everyone who questioned his move. David Puig's runner-up finish in his home region was a nice subplot — the young Spaniard continues to develop into a legitimate talent regardless of which tour's logo is on the scoreboard. Josele Ballester in third adds another name most casual fans won't recognize but should probably start learning.

## What Actually Happened

**Fitzpatrick's iron play was surgical.** Harbour Town's Pete Dye design is essentially a final exam in shot shaping — tight corridors, small greens, trouble everywhere for anyone who misses by more than a few yards. Fitzpatrick's entire skill set was built for this test. His U.S. Open win at Brookline was similar: a course that punished imprecision and rewarded the guy who could put the ball on a string. This wasn't a fluke. This was a course finding its ideal winner.

**Scheffler's post-Masters performance is almost absurd.** Most players treat Heritage week as a recovery lap. Scheffler treated it like a Tuesday practice round that he happened to be competing in — casual excellence, a runner-up finish that he'll probably forget about by Wednesday. The gap between Scheffler's "off weeks" and most players' best weeks continues to widen in uncomfortable ways.

**The Morikawa quiet surge deserves attention.** Fourth place at Harbour Town doesn't generate headlines, but Morikawa has been stacking these finishes with the kind of consistency that usually precedes a breakthrough win. His approach play has been elite for months. When the putter catches up — and it will — he's going to reel off a stretch that reminds everyone of his 2020-2021 peak.

**Rahm is building something in LIV whether we acknowledge it or not.** There's a tendency in golf media to treat LIV results like exhibition scores. But Rahm is clearly playing with intent and intensity. The field depth question remains valid — Puig and Ballester in second and third won't silence skeptics — but Rahm's individual performances are beyond reproach. He's playing some of the best golf of his life with essentially no world ranking points to show for it. That tension isn't going away.

**Bud Cauley in seventh is the kind of result that deserves a second look.** Cauley's career has been defined by talent interrupted — injuries, inconsistency, the cruel randomness of professional golf. A top-ten at Heritage, at a course that demands feel and creativity, suggests he still has stretches of genuinely high-level golf in him.

## Course & History

Harbour Town Golf Links has hosted the Heritage since 1969, making it one of the longest-running non-major events on Tour. The course was designed by Pete Dye with input from Jack Nicklaus, and it remains one of the few Tour stops where length is almost irrelevant. At just over 7,100 yards, it's a throwback — the kind of layout where a well-placed 4-iron matters more than a 340-yard drive. The iconic lighthouse behind the 18th green is one of golf's most recognizable backdrops, and the post-Masters scheduling has given the event a unique identity: a palate cleanser, a place where the game slows down and rewards craft over athleticism.

The Heritage winner's list reads like a hall of fame of precision players — Davis Love III won it five times, and names like Hale Irwin, Nick Faldo, and Tom Watson dot the history. Fitzpatrick's name fits right in.

Over in Mexico, LIV Golf's Playa del Carmen event is still finding its footing historically — only in its third year of existence. But the venue, south of Cancún, offers a resort-golf atmosphere that LIV has leaned into as part of its brand. Whether these tournaments develop the kind of institutional memory that Harbour Town carries remains an open question that only time can answer.

## Looking Ahead: Zurich Classic of New Orleans

The Tour heads to TPC Louisiana for the Zurich Classic, the only official team event on the PGA Tour schedule. The two-man format — alternating between four-ball and foursomes over four days — creates a completely different viewing experience and rewards partnerships that can complement each other's games. It's also one of the more entertaining weeks of the year precisely because it breaks the monotony. Watch for teams that pair a bomber with a wedge wizard, or two elite iron players who can take turns attacking pins. Chemistry matters here in a way it simply doesn't the other 40-something weeks of the season.
