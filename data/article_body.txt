The green jacket slipped over his shoulders for the second consecutive time, and even Augusta National — a place engineered to resist sentimentality — seemed to exhale. Rory McIlroy, the six-time major champion who completed the career Grand Slam here twelve months ago, came back and defended it. He is only the fourth man to win back-to-back Masters, after Jack Nicklaus, Nick Faldo, and Tiger Woods. The margin was a single stroke. It felt like less. It also felt like more.

## The Recap

McIlroy's week was defined not by Sunday but by Friday. His second-round 65 — seven birdies, no bogeys, the kind of ball-striking clinic that makes Augusta look like it's cooperating — built the cushion that ultimately survived 36 hours of erosion. Saturday's 73 was uncomfortable. Sunday's 71 was workmanlike. Neither was pretty. Both were enough.

Scottie Scheffler, the world's top-ranked player and a four-time major champion, authored the weekend's most impressive stretch: 65-68 coming home. The problem was a bewildering second-round 74 that left him four shots behind at the halfway mark. He never quite got close enough to apply real pressure, finishing alone in second at -11.

The T3 logjam at -10 told the week's secondary stories. Cameron Young fired a Saturday 65 that briefly made this a tournament, then gave it all back and then some with a Sunday 73 — the cruelest kind of collapse at a place that remembers everything. Tyrrell Hatton posted the best final round in the field, a 66, but his opening 74 meant he spent the weekend climbing a hill he'd built himself. Russell Henley was steady. Justin Rose — 70-69-69-70 in his mid-40s — was a metronome, four rounds within a single stroke of each other, the kind of consistency that doesn't win Masters but earns standing ovations at the 18th.

Collin Morikawa and Sam Burns shared T7 at -9. Max Homa and Xander Schauffele rounded out the top ten at -8. The cut fell at +2.

## What Actually Happened

**The phones are gone, and it changes everything.** This is the first thing you notice and the last thing you stop thinking about. No screens between you and the golf. No one filming themselves watching. Your senses recalibrate within minutes — suddenly you can hear the thwack of a 7-iron from 200 yards, and you find yourself actually watching the ball instead of the person next to you watching the ball through a 6-inch rectangle. Augusta's phone ban isn't a policy. It's a gift.

**Augusta is mortal, and that's fine.** The greens this year were patchier and browner than the broadcast would ever let you believe. Not a blade out of place? That's the mythology talking. In person, you see seams and dry spots and the kind of imperfections that remind you this is a golf course in Georgia in April, not a soundstage. It doesn't diminish the place. It makes it more impressive, actually — the fact that these surfaces still produce the most demanding putting test in golf while looking slightly tired says more about the design than the agronomy.

**The roars are real, and they travel.** Without the ambient noise pollution of fifty thousand smartphones, every reaction carries across the property in waves. You hear Amen Corner from the first fairway. You know something happened at 15 before the leaderboard updates. McIlroy's birdie at 13 on Friday rippled through the pines like a weather system. The calm doesn't just amplify sound — it creates a communication network. The entire course is eavesdropping on itself.

**The pine corridors reward creativity more than you'd think.** Television compresses the trees into walls. In person, the pines are spaced generously — real corridors exist between them, real angles open up. This is why players who shape the ball thrive here. It's not about avoiding the trees. It's about using the gaps the trees create. McIlroy's ability to work the ball both ways — particularly his high draw on the par-5s — looked even more decisive from ground level than it does on a monitor.

**Fifty thousand people, zero incidents.** The civility is remarkable and maybe a little eerie. Everyone knows how to behave. There's no VIP separation — walked a hole shoulder-to-shoulder with the USGA president — and somehow that egalitarianism breeds its own decorum. People dress for the Masters like it's a cultural event, because it is one. The women, in particular, come prepared. Best-managed sporting event I've attended: five to ten minutes to park, under five to exit at peak time. Everyone is genuinely, palpably happy to be there.

## Course & History

Augusta National Golf Club was born in 1934 from the imagination of Alister MacKenzie and Bobby Jones, built on the grounds of Fruitland Nurseries — a former indigo plantation turned commercial plant operation. MacKenzie died in January 1934 before the first Masters was played that March. Every hole is still named after a plant or tree: Flowering Peach, Pink Dogwood, Yellow Jasmine, Azalea. The botanical heritage isn't decorative. It's structural.

The course now plays approximately 7,545 yards, stretched repeatedly over the decades, but the greens remain MacKenzie's masterpiece — the real defense. Augusta has almost no rough to speak of, which seems generous until you reach those putting surfaces. The trade-off is intentional: you'll find your ball, but good luck getting it close. The 12th hole, Golden Bell — just 155 yards over Rae's Creek — remains statistically the most treacherous hole in major championship history relative to par. A 155-yard par 3 that has decided more Masters than most par 5s.

The intertwined routing is something you only appreciate on foot. Every hole has sightlines to at least one other. The players always know what's happening. It's golf as theater-in-the-round — every actor visible, every plot twist witnessed in real time.

## Looking Ahead: RBC Heritage

The caravan moves 270 miles southeast to Hilton Head Island and a course that could not be more different from what we just left. Harbour Town Golf Links — Pete Dye's 1969 gem — plays under 7,100 yards at par 71. It rewards precision over power, creativity over brute force. Tree-lined fairways, small greens, the iconic lighthouse guarding 18. This is where bombers go to feel uncomfortable and shot-makers go to exhale.

The field thins considerably, as it always does the week after the Masters. Scheffler, McIlroy, and several top-10 finishers from Augusta will sit this one out. That creates opportunity — and the course fit data this week points to some interesting names.
